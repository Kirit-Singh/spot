## 1. Does the SkewScore engine generalize to an arbitrary user-picked A→B?

The honest answer is: **the perturbation-side machinery generalizes cleanly; the axis-side does not, and the memo currently treats the whole engine as if it inherits the generality of the projection primitive.** That's the main thing to fix.

**What genuinely is axis-agnostic (and this is the strong core of the design):**

- **Power gating (§2.3, R4).** Whether a guide actually knocked its target down (`ontarget_significant`, `low_target_gex`, `single_guide_estimate`) is a property of the perturbation, not of what you picked as A and B. It transfers to any contrast without modification. This is load-bearing and correctly identified as such.
- **Cross-guide / cross-donor reproducibility (§2.4).** Same story — sign-concordance of δ_X across guides/donors is computed before any projection and is contrast-independent.
- **The NTC-guide empirical null (§2.5).** This is the most elegant general component. Projecting the NTC pseudobulks onto *any* axis w gives a valid "no real perturbation" null for that axis. It's the right analogue to Stage-1's permutation floor and it holds for arbitrary A/B. Two caveats worth writing down: the null must be **recomputed per contrast** (its variance depends on w's direction — you cannot cache one null across selections), and the achievable BH-adjusted p-value tail is bounded by the number of NTC pseudobulks. Over 3,341 tests you may need a parametric fit to the NTC projection rather than a pure empirical tail; check n_NTC can actually support the multiplicity you're claiming.

**What does NOT generalize, and isn't currently gated:**

**(a) There is no axis-quality gate.** The projection δ_X · ŵ is always computable, but its meaning collapses when A and B are not cleanly separable on the NTC reference. A user can select two nearby substates that differ in a handful of genes; then w is dominated by noise, the sign-agreement filter (scVI∩DESeq2) leaves a thin axis, and the entire screen projects onto a poorly-conditioned direction — silently, because nothing checks it. The default (induced-Treg-48h vs inflammatory-Th1-8h) is a large, well-separated contrast, so the memo never feels this. **Add a gate on the axis itself before any perturbation is scored:** number of genes significantly separating A from B, cross-validated separability of A vs B on held-out NTC cells, and how many genes survive the two-method sign-agreement. If the axis isn't well-defined, the correct output is "this contrast is underpowered as an axis," in the same spirit as your per-perturbation power tiers.

**(b) Cosine-specificity as a sledgehammer discriminator is contrast-dependent — this is the subtle one.** The logic "high cosine = specific program-shifter, low cosine = pan-essential sledgehammer" only works because the Treg→Th1 axis is a *focused* effector-program direction that is largely orthogonal to the housekeeping/ribosomal/cell-cycle directions a pan-essential knockdown moves. If a user picks a contrast that is itself broad and metabolically loaded — the obvious example is anything resembling **Rest vs Stim** — then w has large loadings on exactly the cell-cycle/translation/metabolic genes that a sledgehammer perturbs, and the essential-gene knockdown will show **high** cosine with w. The specificity filter inverts from a guard into a confound. §2.2 and R5 present cosine as a universal discriminator; it is not. At minimum, flag that the cosine defense weakens as the axis broadens, and consider explicitly projecting out (or reporting alongside) a proliferation/translation axis so the sledgehammer signal has somewhere to go that isn't w.

**(c) The positive-control sign gate has no general replacement.** For the default, FOXP3-KD → strongly-positive is a beautiful anchor that fixes the sign and validates the whole method before ranking is trusted (§2.6, R8). R8 acknowledges this must be re-derived per contrast and says to "pick the control whose KD is expected to drive B." The problem is that this quietly assumes such a control *exists and is known* — that for an arbitrary A/B the user can name a master regulator whose knockdown has an established directional effect. For many interesting contrasts (two effector substates, a subtle kinetic split) there is **no canonical master-regulator knockdown**, so the sign is mathematically determined by the A/B labeling but the *validation* that the axis behaves sensibly is simply absent. Don't paper over this: the engine should carry an explicit **"sign validated / sign unvalidated"** state per contrast, and when unvalidated, say so wherever a hit is reported. The default gets a validated sign; a generic contrast often will not, and that materially changes how much trust the ranking earns.

**(d) A confound baked into the shipped default itself — worth raising even though it's not the general-case question.** The default poles live at **different timepoints**: A = induced-Treg at 48h, B = Th1 at 8h. So w conflates a *fate* difference (Treg vs Th1) with an *activation-duration* difference (48h vs 8h). You then score each perturbation's **Stim48hr** δ_X against this axis. A knockdown that merely makes 48h cells look transcriptionally *younger* (less activation-matured, more 8h-like) will project positive without any real Th1 skew. This interacts with R1: the perturbation vector is measured at 48h, one axis pole is defined at 8h. It's a genuine, actionable confound in the flagship example, not an edge case. Mitigations: define both poles at a common timepoint where possible; or regress an explicit activation-duration/kinetics axis out of w; or, at minimum, verify top hits aren't just shifting cells along the 8h↔48h activation trajectory. I'd want this stated in R2/R3 territory rather than left implicit.

**(e) R1 should be generalized from a fixed statement into a per-contrast number.** R1 (whole-condition pseudobulk swamps the induced-Treg subset, which is ~6.3% of cells) is correctly ranked the largest risk. But its severity is a *function of the selected contrast*: it gets worse the smaller A∪B is as a fraction of the scored condition, and it's mild when A and B are the dominant populations. The cheap screen is measuring "shift of the majority population along the axis" as a proxy, and the proxy quality is exactly the A∪B fraction. So compute and report **the fraction of the scored condition's cells belonging to A∪B**, and warn (or refuse the cheap lane) when it's low. That single number tells the user whether the primary metric is even a reasonable proxy for their chosen contrast — which is precisely what a selection-parameterized method needs.

**R2 and R3** are fine as written and generalize correctly (R2 = NTC-axis-applied-to-perturbed-cells is a mean-shift assumption for any contrast; R3 = verify load-bearing genes survive the 10,282 universe for whatever poles are chosen). R2 does get sharper near strong regulatory hubs (perturbations that *reshape* rather than *slide along* the program), but that's a general caveat you already name.

**Net on Q1:** the engine is really two objects wearing one name — a general, sound projection-and-gating framework, and a set of biologically-tuned validators (FOXP3 control, the focused-axis assumption behind cosine) that are Treg→Th1-specific. The generality claim is fully earned for the former and only conditionally for the latter. The fix is not architectural; it's a small set of **axis-level gates** (separability, A∪B fraction, sign-validation state, broad-axis warning for cosine) that run before the per-perturbation screen, mirroring the per-perturbation power tiers you already have.

## 2. Lane C — scLDM-CD4 as a parameterized in-silico prioritizer

**The overall stance is right.** Using a generative model trained on the same data as a *hypothesis prioritizer* behind a hard firewall — measured DE stays ground truth, model proposes, model-only hits stay `suggestive`/`CS-complement`, circularity named explicitly (R9) — is the correct and appropriately humble way to use it. Don't let anything below make you soften that firewall; if anything I'd harden it. But several caveats are incomplete or wrong, and one is a factual error about how the model works.

**Factual correction (the important one): the flow does not transport A→B.** §2.8 says "its optimal-transport / flow design literally models one state distribution moving into another — i.e. the A→B fate transition itself, not just a mean DE shift." That's not what the model does. Per the model's own documentation, the autoencoder learns a latent cell-state representation and the flow-matching module generates perturbed profiles **conditioned on perturbation identity and context, with classifier-free guidance** — a deep generative framework composed of an autoencoder and a flow-matching module, where the flow model generates perturbed expression profiles conditioned on perturbation identity and context variables. Critically, training uses an optimal-transport formulation to define target couplings along the flow starting from random noise, rather than constructing explicit couplings between control and perturbed cells. So the OT coupling is **noise→data**, not control→perturbed and not A→B. The model gives you conditional distributions; an A→B skew has to be *constructed* as a counterfactual difference (generate B-context under knockdown vs under control, compare), it is not something the flow "models directly." This matters two ways: it removes the strongest selling point in §2.8 (the claim that the model captures the transition itself rather than a mean shift — it doesn't, in the way stated), and it means the in-silico SkewScore is a difference of two generated conditionals, which reintroduces exactly the mean-shift character you were hoping to transcend. Reword §2.8 to describe it as **conditional counterfactual generation**, not transport of A into B.

**Missing caveat, and it's the most important one: the model inherits the same confounds as the training data, so concordance cannot rescue R1 or the activation-duration confound.** R9 correctly says the model isn't *independent*. But the memo stops at "not independent" and then hopes the model's design buys back cell-state resolution the screen lacks ("cell-state-resolved effects... gene combinations never directly measured"). If it's trained on the same cells, it learned the same 6.3%-subset sparsity and the same whole-condition structure. A model fit on data where the induced-Treg subset is rare and sparsely perturbed will have a data-starved transport for exactly that subset — so its "cell-state-resolved" prediction there is weakest precisely where you most want it. Add this explicitly: **scLDM inherits R1/R2 and the activation confound, not just non-independence; it cannot supply resolution the training data didn't contain.**

**The combinations claim is out-of-distribution for this checkpoint.** §2.8's "reach the real screen lacks — gene combinations never directly measured" is aspirational. scLDM-CD4 v0.1 is documented as designed to synthesize single-cell mRNA expression profiles of CD4+ T cells under single-gene knockdown perturbations. Combination prediction is a capability of the *base* scLDM on other datasets, not a validated function of the released CD4 checkpoint. Predicting unmeasured combinations is therefore squarely the OOD regime the memo elsewhere says is unreliable — you'd be leaning on the one use the model is least qualified for. Either drop the combinations pitch or label it as an untested extrapolation, not a strength. The reach it *does* legitimately add is interpolation the vendor actually claims: in-silico exploration of perturbed profiles for new donors and time points where empirical training data are limited. That — donor/timepoint interpolation — is the defensible "reach," and it's bounded.

**Gene-space mismatch — a direct parallel to R3 that the memo doesn't call out.** scLDM operates in an HVG space (the base paper works in ~2,000 HVGs for one dataset; the CD4 checkpoint uses its own HVG panel), while your axis and screen live in the 10,282-gene DE universe and 18,130-gene reference. The in-silico skew toward B is therefore computed in a *different, smaller* gene vocabulary — and you need to verify the **load-bearing axis genes survive the model's HVG selection** (does FOXP3 make the HVG cut? CTLA4? IFNG?). This is R3 all over again, one layer removed: if the poles' defining genes aren't in the model's vocabulary, the in-silico lane literally cannot represent the contrast the measured lane uses. Add an explicit HVG-coverage check for the axis genes, mirroring R3.

**The in-silico lane has no null and no significance — an asymmetry with Lane A.** Lane A gets an NTC-guide empirical null and BH-adjusted q-values. Lane C, as described, produces only a rank. So how do you decide an in-silico hit is "real enough" to be promoted to `suggestive`? Top-N of a bare ranking isn't comparable rigor. Give the model lane its own null — generate under NTC/scrambled conditioning, or a label-permutation — so a `model_only` call means "beyond the model's own noise," not just "high on an unnormalized list."

**Separate model-QC from prioritization, and run the positive control through the model.** The memo blurs two different uses of concordance-with-DE. Concordance on single-gene knockdowns is (a) largely circular as a hit signal, exactly as R9 says, but (b) *very* useful as **model validation** — before trusting scLDM as a prioritizer at all, confirm it reproduces the measured positive control (does model-generated FOXP3-KD move toward loss-of-induced-Treg?) and a held-out set of measured KDs. That's the analogue of your §2.6 sign gate, applied to the model. Then reserve the model's *unique* output (donor/timepoint interpolation, prioritization ahead of the expensive compositional confirmation) for where there's no measured competitor and the firewall does the epistemic work. Framed this way, concordance is a gate you must pass to use the model, not evidence you harvest from it — which is both more honest and more useful.

**Parameterization detail: condition on source = A, not just target = B.** §2.8 says "set target = state B" and "desired outcome is the chosen target state B," but the measured SkewScore is *directional* (toward B **and away from A**). Ranking generated cells by proximity to B alone is a different quantity than the signed A→B contrast — a knockdown could land cells near B from an irrelevant starting point. To match the measured lane, the counterfactual must be anchored on the A context. Tighten the spec so the in-silico contrast is the same signed A→B object, not a one-sided "closeness to B."

**One practical/feasibility note the memo omits:** the model has no CPU-only inference — it's tested on A100/H100/A6000 GPUs. Given the local GPU here isn't reachable from the compute environment, Lane C is a remote-GPU job that has to be provisioned. That's fine, but it belongs in the execution/scoping section (step 2b) as a hard dependency, alongside the MIT-license check you already did. Also, cite the training data as **Zhu et al. 2025** (the preprint the checkpoint's inputs are described from) rather than a bare "14.5M cells / 3,699 HVGs" — those specific numbers I could not confirm from the model's public docs, so pin them to the source or soften them before they end up in a provenance block.

**Is it correctly scoped as CS-complement / never in the confirmed set?** Yes, unambiguously, and that's the right call given non-independence. Keep it there.

## If I could change only a few things

1. **Add axis-level gates before the per-perturbation screen** — A/B separability, the A∪B-fraction proxy-quality number (generalize R1), a sign-validation state (generalize R8), and a broad-axis warning that cosine-specificity can invert. This is what actually makes the engine "general" rather than "general-shaped but validated only on Treg→Th1."
2. **Flag the 48h-vs-8h activation confound in the shipped default** and decide how to handle it (common-timepoint poles, or regress out a kinetics axis). It's in your flagship example, not a hypothetical.
3. **Rewrite §2.8's mechanism** — it's conditional counterfactual generation (noise→data flow), not A→B transport; add "inherits the training data's confounds/subset-sparsity" as a first-class caveat; downgrade the combinations claim to untested OOD; add an HVG-coverage check for axis genes and a model-side null; and reframe DE-concordance as a **model-QC gate** rather than a hit signal.

None of this touches the architecture you've designed — the file chain, the program registry, the two-lane cheap/confirmatory split, and the firewall are all sound. These are gates and reframings that make the generality and the generative lane as honest as the rest of the memo already is.
